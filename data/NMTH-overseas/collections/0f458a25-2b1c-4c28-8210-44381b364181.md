## 中文 (TRANSLATION)

##--SPLIT--##
福爾摩沙的蓪草紙

    羅伯特．史溫侯，福爾摩沙領事著。
    用來製作蓪草紙（宣紙）的植物應該是植物學家稱之為蓪草（Aralia papyrifera，目前學名 Tetrapanax papyriferus）的植物，是一種具有大型葉片的低矮灌木，其形態與生產蓖麻油的植物蓖麻（Riciuns communis）差不多。這種植物目前只分布於福爾摩沙島北部，在丘陵地區生長得非常茂盛。它的生長速度非常快，被砍下使用的樹幹和枝條，外觀上與老齡的植株沒什麼不同。蓪草的細胞組織，或稱「髓心」，在第一年就會生長到完整大小。樹幹和枝條大多從內山的原住民（aborigines）那邊買來的，他們用以物易物的方式交換中國人的商品。很少有整株筆直的蓪草，通常會切成大約9英吋長的段狀。從一端插入一根棍子，再往地面敲擊，髓心就會從另一端彈出。接著，將髓心插入直的空心竹管中，髓心會膨脹並直接乾燥。如果因為太短無法形成紙張所需要的寬度，則會將好幾塊髓心一起塞入空心的竹管內，然後在竹子兩端插入杆子，將這些髓心擠壓在一起直到乾燥為止。通過這樣的處理程序，小塊的髓心切段會被迫粘在一起，形成符合所需長度的長直髓心塊。因此，幾乎任何尺寸的紙張都能被製作出來。將髓心削成紙片的刀具，其形狀與屠夫的砍刀相似。它被用石塊磨得十分鋒利，不需要用到的時候，會將刀刃一側放在木製的凹槽中，並用兩根繩子將木頭和刀子綁緊。使用前，人們會在一個小木塊上修整刀刃，這個木塊通常是楠木類樹木（Machilus ramosa，目前無法確定樹種），形狀像塊大骨。而用來切髓心的磚塊，則是由光滑的磚塊或燒製的粘土塊製成。紙張的每個邊上都舖有一塊細窄的黃銅片，用來擺放蓪草刀，因此會稍微比磚塊高一點。磚塊平放於桌面，用左手的手指在磚塊上滾動乾燥的髓心，右手握住刀柄，將刀子的刃面朝向髓心，放在黃銅片上。當右手將蓪草刀往左推時，用左手的手指將髓心往相同的方向滾動，但是速度較慢。這樣一來，就可以不斷地削下髓心片，直到剩下直徑約四分之一英吋的內髓為止。內髓長得有點像小鯊魚的脊柱，並且分成更小的內凹節理。中國人將其作為一種精緻藥材來使用。髓心削製後可產出大約四英呎長的平滑薄片，前六英吋因帶有橫向溝紋，所以被當成無用之物切除。剩下的部分就是一張白色薄片。切成薄片後，會將它們一張一張地疊放在一起並重壓一段時間，接著按照所需尺寸切成正方形。這些裁好的小方形通常會被染上不同的顏色，再製成人造花，作為當地女性的頭飾；由蓪草紙製成的假花簡直栩栩如生。市面上最常見的是素色及未染色的蓪草紙，尺寸大約為31/4英吋見方。每包一百張，一包不到一便士；或者五包一捆，每捆四便士。尺寸較大的紙張需要訂做，通常出口到廣東（Canton）。在廣東，歐洲人對奇特但色彩豐富的蓪草紙畫作一直以來都感到十分好奇。我們有些人試著自己削蓪草紙，儘管在學徒手中看起來一點也不難，但我們大部分的試作都以失敗收場，只製造了一些碎屑。

The Rice-paper of Formosa.
By Robert Swinhoe, H. M. Consul at Formosa.
The plant that perhaps produces the so-called rice-paper is the Aralia papyrifera of botanists, a low shrub with large leaves, in form not unlike those of the castor-oil plant (Ricinus communis). This plant has as yet only been produced from the northern and of Formosa, where it grows wild in great abundance on the hills. It is of very quick growth, and the trunk and branches, which are lopped for use, are not unlike those of an elder in appearance. The cellular tissue or pith attains its full size the first year. The trunks and branches are mostly procured from the aborigines of the inner mountains, in barter for Chinese produce. They are rarely straight throughout their length, and are usually cut into pieces of about nine inches long, and with a straight stick inserted at one end and hammered on the ground, the pith is forced out with a jump at the other end. The pith is then inserted into straight hollow bamboos, where it swells and dries straight. If too short to form the required breadth of paper, several bits are inserted into a hollow bamboo, and, by rods inserted at both ends of the bamboo, pressed together until dry. By this process the short bits are forced to adhere together and form one straight long piece of the required length. Thus paper of almost any size can be produced.l The knife used in paring the pith into paper is in shape not unlike a butcher’s chopper. It is well sharpened on a stone, and, when not used, kept with the edge in a wooden groove held firm to it by two strings round the wood and the knife. Before using it, the edge receives a fresh touch-up on a small block of wood, usually a piece of the timber of Machilus ramosa, shaped like a large bone. The block on which the pith is cut consists of a smooth brick or burnt-clay tile, with a narrow piece of brass on the rim of paper pasted at each edge, on which the knife is laid, and is consequently a little raised above the bare tile itself. The block is laid flat on a table, and the dried pith rolled on it with the fingers of the left hand, and then the knife laid on the braes rims with its edge toward the pith, its handle being held by the right hand. As the knife is advanced leftwards by the right hand, the pith is rolled in the same direction, but more slowly, by the fingers of the left. The paring thus goes on continuously, until the inner pith, about a quarter of an inch in diameter, is left, resembling somewhat the vertebral column of a very small shark, and breaking into smaller concave-sided joints. This is used by the Chinese as an opulent medicine. The paring produces a smooth continuous scroll about four feet long, the first six inches of which are transversely grooved and cut off as useless. The rest shows a flae whitesheet. The sheets, as they are cut, are placed one upon another and pressed for some time, and then cut into squared of the required size. The small squares made here are usually dyed different colors, and manufactured into artificial flowers for the adornment of the hair of the native ladies; and very excellent imitations of flowers they make. The sheets most usually offered for sale, plain and undyed, are about three inches and a quarter square, and are sold in packets of one hundred each, at rather less than one penny the packet, or a bundle of five packets for fourpence the larger XXX paper is made to order, and is usually exported to Canton, whence the grotesque but richly-tinted XXX paper paintings have long attracted the curiosity of Europeans. Some of us tried our hands at paring, but made most abortive attempts, producing only chips, though the operation looked so easy in the hands of the apprentice.
