## Description

史溫侯剛從福爾摩沙回到英格蘭，獲得新種藍鵲（Urocissa）、新種擬啄木（Megalaema，目前屬名Psilopogon）、兩種鉤嘴鶥（Pomatorhini ，目前屬名Pomatorhinus）、三種噪鶥（Garrulaxes）、一種山鷓鴣（Arboricola），雄性與雌性均有，另外有描述尼泊爾的朱鸝（P. traillii）。

## 中文 (TRANSLATION)

##--SPLIT--##
XLII 史溫侯先生來信
致《英國鳥類科學期刊》（The Ibis）編輯
您好，為了轉換環境，我剛從福爾摩沙回到英格蘭。我在那邊的時候頗為積極地投入自然史方面的研究，因此得以從那座尚未被探索過的島上帶回一些不錯的新玩意兒。關於其他鳥類學方面的消息，我想說說一種非常迷人的新種雉雞。此種雉雞屬於鷴類（Euplocomus）的鳥，我很幸運地將其中幾種鳥的雌雄兩性都收集到手──美麗的新種藍鵲（Urocissa）、新種擬啄木（Megalaema，目前屬名Psilopogon）、兩種鉤嘴鶥（Pomatorhini ，目前屬名Pomatorhinus）、三種噪鶥（Garrulaxes）、一種山鷓鴣（Arboricola），以及其他。我打算在11月動物學會的第一次會議時全都帶過去。但在我帶回來的這些新玩意兒中，有一個也許是這之中最美麗的；我很期待能藉由《英國鳥類科學期刊》將牠介紹給鳥類學界。我說的是Psaropholus屬下的黃鸝型（Orioline form），尼泊爾的朱鸝（P. traillii），或另外一種鳥也在這個鳥屬之下。這兩個鳥種都因福爾摩沙的族群顯得黯然失色。我會補上牠的特徵描述，再加上一些我目前可以提供的註記。當我大部分的收藏抵達英格蘭時，我就能夠補充更多的細節。

朱鸝（Pasrophlus ardens, Swinhoe，目前學名Oriolus traillii）（整頁插畫 XIII）
P. coccineus：頭部和頸部是圓的，翅膀和脛骨是深黑色的；喙和腳的鉛灰色的。

##--SPLIT--##
喙部是淺鈷藍色；眼睛周圍的皮膚為紫羅蘭灰色；虹膜深紅棕色；腿部呈鉛灰色；腳底和爪子為暗黑色。頭部、頸部、翅膀以及腿上的羽毛是黑色的；其他的羽毛呈現一種很漂亮的胭脂紅色，尾羽灰白。體長9英吋；翼長6英吋；尾長4英吋，偏圓形，有12根羽毛。上喙長1英吋；下喙邊緣長1英吋。跗蹠長9英吋。
一齡鳥的羽毛呈淡灰色，身體下部帶一點白色，有黑色條紋。
這種鳥棲息在福爾摩沙山區，時常出現於高山山谷中的灌木林，在樟樹（Laurus camphora，目前學名 Cinnamomum camphora）粗壯茂密的枝頭之間展現牠一身俗艷的羽色。樟樹是一種知名的樹，零零星星聳立在盤根錯節的樹林間。朱鸝（Red Oriole）跟牠黃色的同類【即黃鸝】在習性上很相近，都吃漿果，以榕果為主食。夏天時牠會移動到最高的山區，那裡長年覆蓋著白雪（因此我對於牠築巢的事情一無所知）；冬天時牠會回到中國境內最容易到達的山區。牠的叫聲響亮而刺耳，黃鸝（Yellow Oriole，學名：Oriolus chinensis）也是，不過黃鸝的叫聲雖然有些大聲，但不難聽。在福爾摩沙，就跟在中國一樣，黃鸝是夏候鳥，在夏季時，成千上萬隻蜂擁而至，聚集在南部的竹林中。在這個季節，牠遍布在福爾摩沙所有的沿海地區以及郊區。我在中國的時候自己追蹤牠的，最北曾到北京；根據馮‧施倫克（von Schrenk）的說法，牠的分布範圍可達黑龍江。我很肯定牠冬天遷徙到南方時會穿過暹羅（我曾從尚伯克爵士〔Sir R. Schomburgk〕收到過來自此處的標本），然後與牠的同伴四散在整個孟加拉灣西側。布萊思先生（Mr. Blyth）的黃鸝（O. indicus，目前學名 Oriolus chinensis）就是來自於孟加拉灣西側，他現在認為這種鳥跟中國的族群是相同物種。
請原諒我如此岔離這封信的主題。總之，我對這兩種鳥的看法可以用幾句話來總結：Orislus chinensis又名 indicus是一種棲息在平原的候鳥，而Psaropholus ardens是一種福爾摩沙特有的山區鳥種，牠棲息在這座島上，而且隨季節變化遷徙時，只從低海拔遷到高海拔，或是相反。

##--SPLIT--##
這兩種鳥都是鸝類（Oriole）的鳥，習性略為相似，其中一種有粉紅色的喙（Oriolus proper），另一種有藍色的喙（Psaropholus），但兩種都有鉛灰色的腳。
致上最誠摯的問候
羅伯特‧史溫侯
倫敦，1892年9月1日

## 英文 (ORIGINAL)

XLII. — Letter from Mr. Swinhoe
(Plate XIII.)
To the Editor of ‘The Ibis.’
Sir, — I have just returned to England from Formosa, for change of air; and as, while there, I prosecuted my researches in natural history with some vigour, I have been enabled to bring with me some fine novelties from that hitherto unexplored island. Among other things in the ornithological line, I may mention a most lovely new species of Pheasant of the Euplocomus group, of which I was fortunate enough to procure both sexes,—a fine new Urocissa, a new Megalaema, two Pomatorhini, three Garrulaxes, an Arboricola, cum multis aliis. All these I intend bringing before the Zoological Society at their first meeting in November. But with regard to one of the novelties, perhaps the most beautiful thing I have brought, I should like to anticipate the introduction to the ornithological world by availing myself of the pages of ‘The Ibis’ to bring it forward. I refer to the accompanying Orioline form, belonging to the genus Psaropholus, to which P. traillii from Nepal and perhaps one other species belong. Both of these, however, are cast far into the shade by their Formosan representative. I subjoin a diagnosis of its characters, with the few notes I can at present supply. I shall be able to add further particulars when the mass of my collection reaches England.
PSAROPHOLUS ARDENS, SWINHOE. (Pl. XIII.)
P. coccineus; capite et cervice undique cum alis tibiisque nigerrimis; rośtro pedibusque plumbeis.

##--SPLIT--##
Bill light cobalt; skin round the eye violet-grey; iris crimson-brown; legs leaden grey; soles and claws dingy. Head, neck, wings, and tibial feathers black; the rest of the plumage of a fine cochineal red, paler on the tail. Length 9 3/4 in.; wing 6 1/4; tail 4 1/4, somewhat rounded, of 12 feathers. Bill, along culmen, 1 in.; along edge of lower mandible, 1 1/4. Tarsi 9 in.
The plumage of the first year is pale and dingy, the under parts being more or less white, with a few black streaks.
This bird is an inhabitant of the mountain-ranges of Formosa, frequenting the jungly bush of the exalted valleys, and displaying its gaudy tints among the gigantic leafy boughs of the far-famed Laurus camphora, which towers at intervals among its entangled fellows of the wood. In habits the Red Oriole nearly approaches its allies of the yellow group, and feeds, like them, on berries, chiefly those of figs. In summer it resorts to the highest ranges, some of which are perennially covered with snow (hence of its nesting I know nothing); in winter it returns to the most accessible mountains bounding the Chinese territory. Its notes are loud and harsh, as are those of the Yellow Oriole (Oriolus chinensis), which, however, has somewhat of a loud and not disagreeable song. In Formosa, as in China, the Yellow Oriole is a summer visitant, arriving in thousands, and literally swarming in the bamboo-groves of the south. During this season it spreads itself throughout all the seaboard and champaign country of Formosa. In China I have myself traced it as far north as Pekin; and it ranges into Amoorland, according to von Schrenk. I have no doubt that in its southern and brumal migration it passes through Siam (whence I have received specimens from Sir R. Schomburgk), and disperses its bands throughout the western side of the Bay of Bengal, where Blyth’s Black-naped Oriole (O. indicus) hails from. This species Blyth himself now considers identical with the Chinese bird.
You must excuse me for digressing in this way from the subject of this letter; for, after all, my ideas regarding the two birds might be expressed in a very few words, viz. that whereas Oriolus chinensis alias indicus is a bird of the plains and migratory, Psaropholus ardens is a mountainous species peculiar to Formosa, resident on the island, and merely changing its home from a

##--SPLIT--##
lower to a higher elevation, or vice versa, according to the season; that both birds are Orioles, of somewhat similar habits, the one form having pink bills (Oriolus proper), the other having blue bills (Psaropholus), but both leaden-grey legs.
I remain, yours, &c.,
Robert Swinhoe.
London, September 1st, 1862.
